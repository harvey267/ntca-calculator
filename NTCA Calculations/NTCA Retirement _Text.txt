The effect of interest rates, including generalized federal rates or rates related to corporate bonds, on a participant's monthly annuity payment within the Retirement & Security Program (a defined benefit plan) is primarily seen through actuarial calculations required to convert the standard benefit into an optional payment form, rather than being a factor in determining the initial accrued benefit amount.

Here is a specific breakdown focusing on monthly annuity recipients:

### 1. Actuarial Equivalence for Optional Monthly Annuities

The most direct way a defined interest rate affects the monthly annuity amount is when a participant chooses a payment option other than the standard benefit form, such as a **Joint and Survivor Annuity** or a **Life Only Annuity**.

- **Adjustment Mechanism:** When an optional form is selected, the monthly payment is adjusted (increased or decreased) so that the **total benefits payable are actuarially equivalent** to the normal retirement benefit (which is the monthly amount payable in the form of a 10-years certain and life thereafter annuity).
- **The Role of Interest Rate:** This calculation of actuarial equivalence relies on specific interest rate and mortality assumptions applied by the Program’s actuary.
- **Specified Rate for Annuity Options:** For a participant who had an Hour of Service on or after January 1, 2011, the actuarial equivalence calculations between various annuity options (such as different joint and survivor annuities) are based on an actuarial standard of **6% interest** and the mortality table prescribed under section 417(e) of the Code in effect for 2009.


Therefore, for a participant selecting an optional monthly annuity, the fixed **6% interest rate** is the direct factor used in converting their standard accrued benefit into the final monthly dollar amount they receive. This internal rate is insulated from short-term fluctuations in broader market rates.

### 2. Interest Rate Use in Actuarial Adjustments (Early/Late Retirement)

Interest rates also factor into adjustments for when a retiree begins receiving benefits earlier or later than their Normal Retirement Date (age 65), though the plan also uses fixed percentage adjustments:

- **Late Retirement:** If a participant retires on a Late Retirement Date (after age 65), their annualized pension is increased by **0.25% for each month** they delay retirement after age 65. However, the accrued benefit is also guaranteed to be no less than the benefit determined at the Normal Retirement Date, increased by **5% per annum** until payments commence (an implicit interest rate factor).
- **Early Retirement:** If a participant retires early (before age 65), their monthly benefit is typically reduced by 0.25% for each month before age 65, and an additional 0.25% for each month before age 60, unless the Rule-of-85 applies.

### 3. Federal/Corporate Rates for Fund Health (Indirect Effect)

Federal interest rates (such as Treasury yields) and corporate bond market performance do not directly adjust the accrued monthly benefit formula, but they are crucial for the long-term financial health of the pension fund, which underpins the security of all future payments:

- **Plan Funding:** The Plan calculates its overall financial health (Plan Liabilities) using a specified **funding interest rate**. For the plan year beginning in 2024, the estimated liabilities were based on the Plan’s **7.50% funding interest rate**.
- **Investment Holdings:** The Plan holds significant investments in fixed income, including **investment grade debt instruments (21% of total assets)** and **high-yield debt instruments (10% of total assets)**. Changes in corporate bond rates and yields directly impact the return on these assets. If returns are strong, it helps secure the future payment of all benefits; if returns are poor, it stresses the required contribution levels necessary to ensure benefits can be paid.
