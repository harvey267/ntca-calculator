


## Guide to Understanding Your NTCA Pension Lump Sum

### **Executive Summary**

This guide walks you through how your NTCA lump sum is calculated. The most important thing to know is that its value is directly tied to interest rates published by the IRS—when rates go down, your lump sum value goes up. This guide will show you which rates to watch, how they are calculated, and how to estimate your potential benefit. All information is referenced to publicly available sources you can verify yourself, such as IRS websites, revenue rulings, and plan documents.

### **Key References:**

- **NTCA Plan Documents**: Annual Funding Notice, Summary Plan Description (SPD), and Specifications.
- **IRS Revenue Ruling 2007-67**: Explains the rules for lump sum calculations after 2007.

### Section 1: Basics of Defined Benefit Pensions

A defined benefit (DB) pension, like the NTCA Retirement & Security Program, promises a fixed monthly payment (an annuity) for life. This payment is based on your salary, years of service, and a benefit formula. Unlike a 401(k), the employer bears the investment risk. Your normal retirement benefit is calculated as a percentage of your average compensation multiplied by your years of service.

### Section 2: Annuity vs. Lump Sum – Which to Choose?

When you retire, you can choose how to receive your pension benefit. The two main options are a lifetime annuity or a single lump-sum payment.

*rs_program_spd.pdf, page 12*

- **Annuity**: This is a guaranteed monthly check for the rest of your life. Once you start receiving payments, the amount is fixed and is not affected by changes in the market or interest rates14. While dropping interest rates increase the plan's overall liabilities, your personal monthly payment does not change. rs_program_spd.pdf, page 1
- **Lump Sum**: This is a one-time payment equal to the present value of all your future expected annuity payments. The value of this lump sum is: 
    **highly sensitive to interest rates**—when rates go down, lump sum values go up.
    > **Snippet from NTCA Specifications (rs_program_specifications.pdf, Page 48)**: "Under this option a Participant’s benefit shall be paid in a single sum equal to the present value of the Participant’s accrued benefit." 
    

> Key Takeaway: Rates Go Down, Lump Sums Go Up 📉🔼
> 
> The single most important factor you can track is the IRS Minimum Present Value Segment Rates. A drop in these rates before your retirement calculation period will result in a higher lump sum payment.

### Section 3: Historical Context – GATT Rates (Pre-2008)

Before 2008, lump sums used the "GATT rate," which was based on the interest rate of 30-year U.S. Treasury securities. This single interest rate was used to calculate the present value of a pension. The Pension Protection Act of 2006 replaced this system.

---

### Section 4: The Pension Protection Act of 2006 (PPA) – Shift to Segment Rates

The PPA reformed lump sum calculations to better reflect market realities, replacing the single GATT rate with three "segment rates" based on high-quality corporate bond yields.

> **Snippet from Pension Protection Act of 2006 (PLAW-109publ280.pdf, Page 142)**: "The term ‘applicable interest rate’ means the adjusted first, second, and third segment rates applied under rules similar to the rules of section 303(h)(2)(C)..." 

The transition from the GATT rate to the new segment rates was phased in over five years, from 2008 to 2012.

---

### Section 5: How Segment Rates Work: From Corporate Bonds to Your Calculation

The interest rates that determine the size of your lump sum are called "segment rates." Here is the step-by-step process of how those rates are created and applied:

Step 1: The IRS Gathers Corporate Bond Data

The process starts with corporate bonds. The IRS is required by law to look at the yields (the rate of return) on high-quality, investment-grade corporate bonds. (PLAW-109pub280.pdf)

Step 2: The Bonds are Grouped into Three "Segments" by Maturity

The law divides all of these bond yields into three groups, or "segments," based on when the bonds are scheduled to mature. (PLAW-109pub280.pdf)

- **First Segment**: Bonds that mature in **0 to 5 years**28. These rates are used to calculate the present value of the first five years of your expected annuity payments.
- **Second Segment**: Bonds that mature in **more than 5 years but not more than 20 years**30. These rates are used for the next fifteen years of your payments.
- **Third Segment**: Bonds that mature in **more than 20 years**. These long-term rates are used for all remaining payments.

Step 3: A Single Interest Rate is Calculated for Each Segment (PLAW-109pub280.pdf)

For each segment, the IRS determines a single representative interest rate for that month34. This is the rate that will be published and used by pension plans.

Step 4: The IRS Publishes Two Sets of Rates (and why the difference is critical)

This is the most important and often confusing part. The IRS publishes two different sets of segment rates each month, and you must know which one applies to your lump sum.

1. **Pension Plan _Funding_ Segment Rates**: These rates are based on a **24-month average** of corporate bond yields363636363636363636. They are used by the NTCA plan for its own internal accounting to determine how much money it needs to set aside to fund all benefits over the long term37. This is the rate referenced on the IRS's "Pension Plan Funding Segment Rates" page.
2. **Minimum Present Value Segment Rates**: These are the rates that matter for **your lump sum calculation**. They are "spot" rates, meaning they are determined
    **without the 24-month averaging**38. This makes them more volatile and directly reflective of the bond market in the specific month your plan uses for its calculation (your "lookback month")39. This is the rate on the IRS's "Minimum Present Value Segment Rates" page.
    

> Key Distinction: Your lump sum is a "present value" calculation governed by Internal Revenue Code §417(e). Your plan documents confirm this.
> 
> Snippet from NTCA Specifications (rs_program_specifications.pdf, Page 48):
> 
> _"Lump sum payment... a Participant's benefit will be equal to the actuarial equivalent of the Participant's annualized pension when computed on the basis of (i) the interest rate determined under section §417(e)(3) of the Code..."_ 
> 
> This confirms that the Minimum Present Value Segment Rates are the correct ones to watch for your personal calculation, not the funding rates.

### Section 6: Mortality Tables – The Second Key Ingredient

After the segment rates, the second key component in a lump sum calculation is the

**applicable mortality table** published by the IRS. This table statistically estimates longevity and is essential for determining the "present value" of your lifetime annuity.

How it Works: An Analogy 🔢

Imagine your pension is a series of monthly payments stretching out for decades. A payment due to you in 30 years is less valuable today than a payment due next month. The segment rates are used to discount that future payment to its present value.

However, the plan also has to account for the probability that you will be there to receive that payment in 30 years. This is where the mortality table comes in.

- The table provides a probability factor for each future year. For example, it might say there is a 99% chance you'll receive payments next year, but only a 25% chance you'll receive a payment 30 years from now.
- Each of your future discounted monthly payments is multiplied by that probability factor.
- The result is that payments expected far in the future, which have a lower probability of being paid, contribute much less to the final lump sum total than payments expected in the near future.


Which Table is Used?

The law requires the use of a specific unisex mortality table for §417(e) lump sum calculations. Your plan documents confirm NTCA follows this rule. The IRS updates this table periodically to reflect increasing life expectancies454545. An increase in longevity generally leads to a slight increase in lump sum values, as the plan must account for a longer potential payout period.

### Section 7: NTCA-Specific Rules

Your NTCA pension has specific rules outlined in the Plan Specifications and Summary Plan Description that govern how these national laws are applied.

- **Calculation Method:** Your lump sum is the present value of your accrued benefit, determined using the §417(e) segment rates and mortality table.
- **Lookback Month:** The specific lookback month is crucial. The NTCA Specifications state the plan uses the segment rates from
    **"the month of August immediately preceding the calendar year that includes the annuity starting date"**. This means for any retirement in 2025, the rates from August 2024 will be used. (rs_program_specifications.pdf, page 48)
- **Lump Sum Options:** You can elect a full lump sum or a combination of a partial lump sum and a smaller monthly annuity. (rs_program_specifications.pdf, page 49)

> Myth vs. Fact: The 7.50% Rate
> 
> **Myth:** My lump sum will be calculated using the 7.50% rate mentioned in the Annual Funding Notice. (rs_program_annual_funding_notice.pdf)
> 
> **Fact:** The 7.50% rate is used only for the plan's internal accounting and funding valuation. Your personal lump sum payout is calculated using the variable IRS segment rates required by law under IRC §417(e). (rs_program_annual_funding_notice.pdf)

> Action Step actionable
> 
> Contact the NTCA plan administrator to confirm the lookback month and stability period rules. This tells you exactly which month's IRS rates will apply to your retirement date.

### Section 8: Step-by-Step Guide to Estimating Your Lump Sum

Estimating your lump sum requires putting all the pieces together: your personal annuity amount, the correct IRS segment rates, and the mortality table. Here is a step-by-step process using a hypothetical example.

Step 1: Calculate Your Monthly Annuity

Before anything else, you need the foundational number: your single life monthly annuity payable at age 6554. You can get an official estimate from NTCA, or you can approximate it yourself using this formula from the plan documents: (rs_program_spd.pdf, page 12)

- **Formula**: (Your Plan's Benefit %) x (High-5 Compensation) x (Years of Service) = Annual Annuity56.
- Example: Let's assume a participant has a 1.9% benefit formula, $80,000 High-5 Compensation, and 25 years of service.
    0.019×$80,000×25 years=$38,000 per year, or $3,166.67 per month.

This monthly annuity is the benefit stream whose "present value" will become your lump sum.

Step 2: Find the Correct IRS Segment Rates

Your lump sum is sensitive to the timing of your retirement because the interest rates can change monthly.

1. **Identify your lookback month.** Per the NTCA Specifications, for a retirement anytime in 2025, the rates from **August 2024** are used. (rs_program_spd.pdf, page 48)
2. **Go to the IRS Website.** Navigate to the "Minimum Present Value Segment Rates" page ([https://www.irs.gov/retirement-plans/minimum-present-value-segment-rates](https://www.irs.gov/retirement-plans/minimum-present-value-segment-rates)).
3. **Find the table for August 2024.** You will use the three segment rates listed for that month.

Step 3: Apply the Rates and Mortality Table to Your Annuity

The actual calculation is a complex actuarial process, but conceptually, this is what happens:

- A calculation is performed for every future monthly annuity payment you are expected to receive.
- Each future payment is "discounted" back to its value in today's dollars using the segment rates. Payments in the first 5 years are discounted with the Segment 1 rate, the next 15 years with the Segment 2 rate, and the remainder with the Segment 3 rate.
- Each of those discounted payments is then multiplied by the probability that you will be alive to receive it, based on the IRS's applicable mortality table.
- Finally, the present values of all those probable future payments are added together to arrive at your final lump sum amount.

### Section 9: Practical Considerations

- **Falling Rates**: If you anticipate that segment rates will drop, and your plan rules allow, delaying your retirement commencement date could result in a higher lump sum. For NTCA, since the rate is set for the entire year based on the previous August, this means timing your retirement
    
    _year_ is more important than the month.
    
- **Taxes**: A lump sum is fully taxable in the year you receive it unless you roll it over directly into an IRA or another qualified plan.
- **PBGC Guarantee**: If the NTCA plan were to terminate without enough money to pay all benefits, your benefit is insured by the Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation (PBGC). For 2024, the maximum guaranteed pension benefit is
    
    **$7,107 per month** for a retiree at age 65. This amount is lower if you retire earlier or elect a survivor benefit. (rs_program_annual_funding_notice.pdf, page 2-3)

### Glossary of Terms

- **Annuity**: A series of fixed monthly payments for life.
- **Applicable Interest Rate**: The set of three segment rates used to calculate your lump sum's present value. (rr-07-67.pdf, page 2)
- **CSEC Plan**: A Cooperative and Small Employer Charity plan, which has different funding rules than standard single-employer plans. (rs_program_annual_funding_notice.pdf)
- **Lookback Month**: The month whose interest rates are used for a given stability period. For NTCA, it is August of the preceding year. (rs_program_specifications.pdf, page 48)
- **Mortality Table**: A statistical table used to predict life expectancy, which is used to determine the probability of making future payments.
- **Present Value**: The value in today's dollars of a future stream of payments, calculated using the applicable interest and mortality tables.
- **Segment Rates**: The three interest rates, based on corporate bond yields, used to discount short-term (0-5 yrs), medium-term (5-20 yrs), and long-term (>20 yrs) payments.
- **Stability Period**: The period during which a set of interest rates remains constant. For NTCA, this appears to be the calendar year.
