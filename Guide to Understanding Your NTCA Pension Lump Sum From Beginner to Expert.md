# Guide to Understanding Your NTCA Pension Lump Sum: From Beginner to Expert

This guide is designed to educate participants in the NTCA Retirement & Security Program (a defined benefit pension plan) on how lump sum retirements are calculated, valued, and affected by interest rates. It starts with basic concepts and builds to advanced topics, including how to interpret IRS data and estimate your benefit. All information is referenced to publicly available sources you can verify yourself, such as IRS websites, revenue rulings, and plan documents. Snippets from key references are included for clarity.

Key references:
- **NTCA Plan Documents**: Annual Funding Notice (rs_program_annual_funding_notice.pdf), Summary Plan Description (SPD) (rs_program_spd.pdf), and Specifications (rs_program_specifications.pdf). Contact NTCA at (828) 252-9776 or www.ntca.org for full copies.
- **IRS Revenue Ruling 2007-67** (rr-07-67.pdf): Explains post-2007 rules for lump sum calculations.
- **Pension Protection Act of 2006 (PPA)** (PLAW-109publ280.pdf): Public Law 109-280, which reformed pension funding and lump sum rules.
- **IRS Websites**: 
  - Minimum Present Value Segment Rates: https://www.irs.gov/retirement-plans/minimum-present-value-segment-rates (for lump sum calculations under IRC §417(e)).
  - Pension Plan Funding Segment Rates: https://www.irs.gov/retirement-plans/pension-plan-funding-segment-rates (related to plan funding, but sometimes relevant for lump sums).
- **Current Mortality Tables**: IRS Notice 2024-42 (search IRS.gov for "Notice 2024-42" or visit https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-24-42.pdf).

**Note**: The NTCA plan is a Cooperative and Small Employer Charity (CSEC) plan, which uses a fixed 7.50% interest rate for funding liabilities (as noted in the 2024 Annual Funding Notice). However, lump sums are calculated using IRC §417(e) segment rates and mortality tables, not the funding rate.

## Section 1: Basics of Defined Benefit Pensions
A defined benefit (DB) pension, like the NTCA Retirement & Security Program, promises a fixed monthly payment (annuity) for life based on your salary, service years, and a benefit formula. Unlike 401(k)s, the employer (or cooperative like NTCA) bears the investment risk.

- **From NTCA SPD (rs_program_spd.pdf, Page 10, Section II)**: "Your normal retirement benefit is calculated as a percentage of your average compensation multiplied by your years of service." Examples in Section XI show how benefits accrue.

***Tip**: Request your personalized benefit statement from NTCA to see your accrued monthly annuity. This is the starting point for any lump sum calculation.

## Section 2: Annuity vs. Lump Sum – Which to Choose?
- **Annuity**: A lifetime monthly payment. Unaffected by market interest rates once started – it's fixed (no cost-of-living adjustments in NTCA plan unless specified).
  - **Impact of Interest Rates**: Dropping rates increase plan liabilities (future payments cost more to fund today), but your monthly amount stays the same. From NTCA Annual Funding Notice (rs_program_annual_funding_notice.pdf, Page 1): "The estimated December 31, 2024, liabilities are based on... the Plan's 7.50% funding interest rate," showing how rates affect overall plan health but not individual annuities.
- **Lump Sum**: A one-time payment equal to the present value of your future annuity stream. This is sensitive to interest rates – lower rates mean higher lump sums (inverse relationship).
  - **From NTCA Specifications (rs_program_specifications.pdf, Page 48)**: "Lump sum payment - Payments shall be made in the form of a single sum. Under this option a Participant’s benefit shall be paid in a single sum equal to the present value of the Participant’s accrued benefit."
  - **Pros/Cons**: Lump sum gives control (invest/rollover to IRA), but you assume longevity and investment risk. Annuity provides guaranteed income.

***Tip**: If rates drop (e.g., Fed cuts), lump sums rise. As of September 24, 2025, check current segment rates on IRS sites to time your election if allowed.

## Section 3: Historical Context – GATT Rates (Pre-2008)
Before 2008, lump sums used the "GATT rate" (Government Average Treasury Thirty-year rate), based on 30-year U.S. Treasury bond yields.

- **Snippet from Rev. Rul. 2007-67 (rr-07-67.pdf, Page 2)**: "For plan years beginning prior to January 1, 2008, § 417(e)(3)(A)(ii)(II) defines the term 'applicable interest rate' as the annual rate of interest on 30-year Treasury securities for the month before the date of distribution..."
- **Lookback/Stability**: Plans held the rate constant for a "stability period" (e.g., month, quarter, year) based on a "lookback month" (1-5 months prior).

**Why It Matters**: If your plan references old rules, but NTCA uses post-2008 segment rates. Verify via SPD.

## Section 4: The Pension Protection Act of 2006 (PPA) – Shift to Segment Rates
PPA reformed lump sum calculations to better reflect market realities, replacing GATT with three "segment rates" based on corporate bond yields.

- **Snippet from PPA (PLAW-109publ280.pdf, Page 142)**: "The term ‘applicable interest rate’ means the adjusted first, second, and third segment rates applied under rules similar to the rules of section 303(h)(2)(C) for the month before the date of the distribution..."
- **Transition**: Phased in over 5 years (2008-2012), blending old GATT with new segments.
  - **Snippet from Rev. Rul. 2007-67 (rr-07-67.pdf, Page 2)**: "§ 417(e)(3)(D)(ii) provides a transition rule that phases in the use of the segment rates over five years."
- **For NTCA**: As a CSEC plan, funding differs, but lump sums follow §417(e) segments (confirmed via web search on CSEC plans: Standard §417(e) applies to lump sums).

***Tip**: PPA aimed to stabilize funding; segment rates make lump sums more market-aligned than the pre-2008 Treasury bond (GATT) rate system.

## Section 5: How Segment Rates Work – Corporate Bonds, Calculation, and Interpretation
Segment rates are discount rates for calculating lump sum present value, divided into three "segments" based on when benefits are paid.

- **Basis on Corporate Bonds**: Derived from yields on high-quality corporate bonds (AAA to BBB ratings).
  - **Snippet from IRS Funding Segment Rates Page (https://www.irs.gov/retirement-plans/pension-plan-funding-segment-rates)**: "The segment rates are based on the averages of yields over different blocks of maturity periods from Treasury high-quality corporate bond yield curves, averaged over 24 months."
  - **Important**: The IRS uses "Treasury" here because the U.S. Treasury Department publishes the HQM yield curve data — not because the rates are based on government treasury bonds. These are high-quality corporate bond yields (AAA to BBB). This is the distinction NTCA failed to communicate clearly.
  - For lump sums (§417(e)): Uses "spot" rates (monthly average, no 24-month smoothing post-transition).
    - **Snippet from IRS Minimum Present Value Page (https://www.irs.gov/retirement-plans/minimum-present-value-segment-rates)**: "For plan years beginning after December 31, 2007, the segment rates are computed without regard to a 24-month average."
- **The Three Segments**:
  - First: Benefits paid in 0-5 years (short-term).
  - Second: 5-20 years (medium-term).
  - Third: >20 years (long-term).
- **24-Month Averaging**: Not used for §417(e) lump sums (spot monthly), but funding (§430) uses 24-month averages with a "corridor" (min/max based on 25-year averages).
  - **Snippet from IRS Funding Page**: "The 24-month average segment rates are calculated by averaging the yields of the corporate bond yield curves over the preceding 24 months."
- **Adjustments/Applicable Percentages**: For funding, a corridor (e.g., 95%-105% for 2020-2030) prevents extreme volatility.
- **Lookback and Stability Periods** (Key for Timing):
  - Stability: Rate fixed for a period (month, quarter, year).
  - Lookback: 1-5 months prior to stability period start.
  - **Snippet from Rev. Rul. 2007-67 (rr-07-67.pdf, Page 2)**: "Section 1.417(e)-1(d)(4) generally provides that a plan must provide for the applicable interest rate to be held constant for a stability period... and that the applicable interest rate for each stability period is... for a specified lookback month that is the first, second, third, fourth, or fifth full calendar month preceding the first day of the stability period."
- **Interpreting IRS Tables**:
  - Columns: Month/Year, First Segment, Second, Third.
  - Example (from IRS Minimum Present Value Page, as of Sept 2025 browse):
    
| Month/Year | First Segment | Second Segment | Third Segment |
|------------|---------------|----------------|---------------|
| Aug-25     | 4.20%         | 5.29%          | 6.08%         |
| Jul-25     | 4.38%         | 5.41%          | 6.13%         |
| ...        | ...           | ...            | ...           |

  - **How to Read**: For a lump sum in October 2025 with a 2-month lookback (August), use Aug-25 rates. Younger retirees (longer payout) are more affected by third segment.
- **Impact of Dropping Rates**: Lower segments increase lump sum (e.g., 1% drop ~10-20% higher sum for 60-year-old).

***Tip**: Visit IRS links monthly; rates update around the 15th.

## Section 6: Mortality Tables – Factoring in Longevity
Lump sums use mortality tables to estimate life expectancy.

- **Applicable Table under §417(e)**: Unisex blend for lump sums.
  - **Snippet from Rev. Rul. 2007-67 (rr-07-67.pdf, Page 4)**: "The § 417(e)(3) applicable mortality table for 2008 is... a fixed blend of 50 percent of the static male combined mortality rates and 50 percent of the static female combined mortality rates..."
- **2025 Table**: Per IRS Notice 2024-42, based on RP-2014 projected with MP-2021 improvements.
  - Search IRS.gov for "Notice 2024-42" for full table (lx and qx values by age).
- **Updates**: Annual, reflecting improved longevity (increases lump sums slightly).

***Tip**: Tables are "static" (projected to year) or generational; NTCA uses static per specs.

## Section 7: NTCA-Specific Rules
- **Lump Sum Calculation**: Present value using §417(e) segments and mortality.
  - **Snippet from NTCA Specifications (rs_program_specifications.pdf, Page 48)**: "A Participant’s benefit shall be paid in a single sum equal to the present value of the Participant’s accrued benefit... determined under Article IV.C(3)(f) [using §417(e)]."
- **Options**: Lump sum if >$1,000 (with rollover), or combo annuity/lump sum.
  - **Snippet from NTCA SPD (rs_program_spd.pdf, Page 21)**: "If the present value of your accrued benefit is $1,000 or less, it will be paid as a lump sum..."
- **Annuity Impact**: Fixed; rates affect plan funding (86.51% funded in 2024 per notice).
- **CSEC Nuance**: Funding at 7.50%, but lump sums use segments.

**Action Step**: Contact NTCA administrator for your plan's lookback (e.g., July for August calc).

## Section 8: Estimating Your Lump Sum
1. Get your monthly annuity estimate from NTCA.
2. Use online calculator (e.g., search "pension lump sum calculator") with current segments and mortality.
3. Formula: PV = Sum of [Annual Benefit / 12 * (1 / (1 + rate)^t)] for each month, adjusted by segments/mortality.
   - Approximate: For age 65, $1,000/month annuity ~ $150,000-$200,000 lump sum at 4-6% rates.

**Tip**: Model in Excel; use IRS tables for precision.

## Section 9: Practical Considerations in a Dropping Rate Environment
- Delay election if rates falling (higher sum), if plan allows.
- Taxes: Lump sum taxable unless rolled to IRA.
- PBGC Guarantee: Up to ~$7,432/month at 65 (2025).
